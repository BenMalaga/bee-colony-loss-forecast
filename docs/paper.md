# Does bee-colony monitoring forecast loss, or only explain it? A pre-registered out-of-sample test on USDA NASS data

*Author: Ben Malaga. Pre-registered 2026-06-10 (commit `43ea36b`, before any outcome data was fetched). Target categories: cs.LG / stat.AP.*

## Abstract

The United States Department of Agriculture surveys honey-bee colony health every quarter and records, for each state, the share of colonies lost and the prevalence of the stressors thought to drive that loss: Varroa mites, disease, pesticides, and others. A prominent line of work fits explanatory models to these data and reports strong in-sample associations between stressors and loss; Insolia et al. (2022), the flagship national study, report an in-sample R² of roughly 0.60. We ask a question that explanatory fit cannot answer. Given only what is known at the end of a quarter, do the surveyed stressor prevalences help *forecast* colony loss in the following quarter, or do they merely co-occur with loss that is already underway? We assemble a state-by-quarter panel from the survey (46 reporting units, namely 45 states plus a NASS "Other States" aggregate, 2015–2025), lock a temporal train/test split (train through 2022, test on 2023–2025), and compare models against seasonal-naive and persistence baselines. Adding the stressor prevalences to a model that already knows past loss produces no statistically detectable improvement in out-of-sample accuracy: the change in skill has a 95% confidence interval spanning zero, and its sign is not stable across model class (gradient boosting versus penalized linear regression), target definition (the integer-rounded published loss percentage versus a continuous reconstruction), or choice of stressor subset. The only component of the forecast that beats the seasonal-naive baseline is a recalibration of that baseline — shrinking the noisy year-ago value toward its conditional mean — rather than any information in recent loss or in the stressors. We report power explicitly. Under the pre-registered primary (state-block) bootstrap the minimum detectable effect is about 0.09 MASE, so the finding is best read as the absence of a *usable* leading signal at state-by-quarter resolution rather than as evidence of no relationship, and it concerns the marginal forecasting value of a coarse, noisy survey measure rather than the causal role of any stressor. The result is a concrete instance of the gap between explanation and prediction (Shmueli, 2010): a strong in-sample association that does not survive an honest out-of-sample test. We release the cleaned panel and a frozen forecasting benchmark so that the floor we establish can be tested and, ideally, beaten.

## 1. Introduction

Public concern about pollinators tends to collapse two very different facts into a single slogan. The global stock of *managed* honey-bee colonies has risen over recent decades, tracking demand for pollination services rather than declining toward extinction (FAOSTAT). At the same time, beekeepers in the United States report severe annual turnover: the 2024–25 US Beekeeping Survey estimated that 55.6% of managed colonies were lost over the year and 40.2% over winter, the highest rates since that survey began in 2010 (Apiary Inspectors of America et al., 2025). The managed-bee problem that is actually policy-relevant, then, is not disappearance but the cost and labor of continually replacing dead colonies. A natural question follows: can the monitoring infrastructure that the public already funds — the USDA's quarterly stressor survey — anticipate loss early enough to be acted on?

The distinction between explaining an outcome and predicting it is not a technicality. A model can identify variables that are genuinely, even causally, associated with an outcome and still forecast poorly, because explanatory modeling and predictive modeling optimize different things and tolerate different errors (Shmueli, 2010). Honey-bee colony loss is a useful test case because the explanatory literature is unusually developed. Insolia et al. (2022, *Scientific Reports*) combine the NASS stressor panel with weather and land-cover covariates and fit a robust in-sample regression with an R² near 0.60, interpreting the stressors as drivers of loss. The most directly predictive prior work, Calovi et al. (2021) and Gray, Goslee, Kammerer & Grozinger (2024), trains random forests but evaluates them with k-fold cross-validation and explicitly declines temporal, train-past/test-future validation, the later study reasoning that a single held-out year is not a good test set and the earlier one that weather differed too much across its three years. That caution is reasonable, but it leaves the forecasting question open: no published study has run a pre-registered, out-of-sample forecasting test on these data against naive baselines, with an explicit contrast between leading and merely coincident signals. That is the gap this paper fills.

We make four contributions. We define a pre-registered out-of-sample forecasting benchmark for state-level quarterly colony loss on public NASS data, with a locked temporal split and locked decision rules, and we show on it that the surveyed stressor prevalences carry no usable marginal forecasting signal, a finding stable across model class, target definition, and feature subset and accompanied by an explicit characterization of the test's power. We then diagnose where the modest predictability that does exist comes from, namely recalibration of the seasonal-naive forecast rather than recent-loss momentum or the stressors. Finally, we release the cleaned panel and the frozen benchmark so that others can submit forecasters against a fixed baseline.

## 2. Data

The data come from the USDA NASS *Honey Bee Colonies* survey, retrieved through the public Quick Stats 2.0 API. After cleaning we have one row per reporting unit, year, and quarter, covering 2015 through 2025, for a total of 1,776 unit-quarters across 46 reporting units: the 45 individual states NASS reports plus an "Other States" aggregate (coded OT) that bundles states too small to publish separately. Hawaii appears only in the earlier years and drops out of the 2023–2025 test window, which therefore contains 45 units. We treat each reporting unit, OT included, as a consistent series over time, which is what the seasonal-naive baseline and the lag construction require. The forecasting target is the published quarterly colony loss percentage (the NASS item "LOSS, DEADOUT, MEASURED IN PCT OF COLONIES"). As a cross-check we reconstruct the percentage as lost colonies divided by maximum colonies for the quarter; the two agree up to NASS's integer rounding of the published figure, and we use the continuous reconstruction in a robustness analysis. The predictors are the six surveyed stressor prevalences — Varroa mites, pests other than Varroa (whose two NASS spellings we unify into a single column), disease, pesticides, other causes, and unknown causes — together with colony inventory, additions, and renovations.

Two features of the data shape the analysis. NASS suppresses small or sensitive cells, marking them `(D)` or `(Z)`; we treat these as missing. The missingness is not negligible and falls mostly on the stressor columns (disease 11.8%, pesticides 8.1%, unknown causes 7.5%), while Varroa prevalence is nearly complete at 0.2% missing; the full table is released as `results/missingness.csv`. The series also contains a genuine gap in 2019 from a federal funding lapse that interrupted the survey, and the 2024 and 2025 years are partial. The cleaned panel is released as `results/bee_colony_panel.csv`, and the full ingestion pipeline is reproducible from the API through `src/fetch_nass.py` and `src/build_panel.py`.

## 3. Methods

All methods below were fixed in the pre-registration before any outcome data was fetched; deviations introduced during analysis are logged in the pre-registration's deviations section and labeled there.

**Target and split.** The unit of analysis is the state-quarter and the target is quarterly loss percentage. We train on all quarters through 2022 and evaluate on the held-out 2023–2025 window. We use no random splits anywhere, since a random split would let the model see the future of a state it is asked to forecast. The forecast horizon is one quarter.

**Predictors and leakage control.** Every forecasting model uses only lagged predictors, at lags of one and four quarters, so that no information from the quarter being predicted enters the model. Lags are built by joining each row to the same state's value four (or one) quarters earlier, so the 2019 gap produces missing lags rather than silently pulling an adjacent quarter into the wrong slot. Same-quarter stressor values are reserved exclusively for the leading-versus-coincident contrast (H2) and are never used as a forecaster.

**Baselines.** Three baselines define the floor a useful forecaster must clear: a seasonal-naive forecast that repeats the value from four quarters earlier, a persistence forecast that repeats the previous quarter, and a per-state climatological mean computed on the training period only.

**Models.** We fit gradient-boosted regression trees (scikit-learn's HistGradientBoosting, which handles missing values natively and so avoids confounding the result with an imputation choice) and a penalized linear model (ridge and LASSO with cross-validated penalty). Per-state classical time-series models are left to future work. All tuning uses training-period data only; the held-out window is scored once.

**Metric and uncertainty.** We summarize accuracy with a seasonal-naive skill ratio, the mean absolute error of a method divided by the mean absolute error of the seasonal-naive forecast on the same held-out set, which we write as MASE for brevity; a value below one beats the baseline. We note that this is an out-of-sample relative-MAE skill ratio rather than the in-sample-scaled MASE of Hyndman and Koehler, applied identically to every method so that all comparisons are fair. Uncertainty comes from two block bootstraps that respect the panel's two correlation structures: a state-block bootstrap that resamples whole states (2,000 resamples) and a period-block bootstrap that resamples whole quarters (1,000 resamples). The state-block bootstrap is the pre-registered primary, since cross-state variation dominates the panel.

**Decomposition and hypotheses.** The headline quantity is a decomposition: the difference between the error of a loss-only model and the error of a loss-plus-stressors model, which is positive when the stressors help. The locked decision rules are: for H1, confirm the null if the lagged-stressor model's skill ratio is at least 0.95 with a confidence interval including one, and refute it if the ratio is below 0.90 with an interval entirely below one; for H2, judge the field's signal coincident rather than leading if a same-quarter model improves on the best lagged model by at least 20%; and for H3, judge weather and land-cover covariates to add no forecasting value if they improve the skill ratio by less than 0.05. H3 requires covariates whose public release does not overlap our test window and is deferred to future work.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Where the skill comes from

On the held-out 2023–2025 window (349 state-quarters across 45 states), the baselines behave as expected for a strongly seasonal series. Persistence is markedly worse than the seasonal-naive forecast (skill ratio 1.230), and the per-state climatological mean essentially ties it (1.003). A gradient-boosted model given only lagged loss does beat the baseline, reaching 0.903 with a 95% confidence interval of [0.831, 0.986] that excludes one. The natural reading is that recent loss carries forecasting information. The decomposition of that skill does not support it. A model given only the single seasonal lag, loss four quarters earlier, reaches 0.908 [0.839, 0.992], and a two-parameter linear recalibration of the seasonal-naive forecast reaches 0.928 on its own. In other words, almost all of the sub-one skill is mean-reversion: the year-ago value is noisy, and shrinking it toward its conditional mean improves it. Adding the recent-quarter term to the seasonal term buys about 0.005 MASE. This becomes the relevant reference point for the rest of the analysis: the bar a stressor signal must clear is not the raw seasonal-naive forecast but its recalibrated version (Figure 1).

### 4.2 Stressors add no detectable forecasting value

Adding all six stressor prevalences (at both lags) to the loss-only gradient-boosted model moves the skill ratio from 0.903 to 0.934, with a confidence interval of [0.843, 1.038]. By the pre-registered rule this leaves H1 inconclusive, and the point estimate moves in the wrong direction: the stressors slightly worsen the forecast. The decomposition is stable across specifications (Table 1). Across the integer-published and continuous targets and across the gradient-boosted and penalized-linear models, the difference in error from adding stressors is small and its confidence interval always includes zero, and its sign is not even consistent: the linear model nudges it positive, the tree model nudges it negative. The negative sign under gradient boosting is best read not as "stressors hurt" but as the tree model expending capacity on mostly-suppressed, noisy columns; the penalized linear model, which can zero those columns out, simply finds nothing.

### 4.3 Leading versus coincident

If the stressors carry no *leading* signal, one might still expect them to track *concurrent* loss strongly, which would show up as a same-quarter model that clearly beats the lagged models. It does not. A gradient-boosted model given the same-quarter stressor values — not a valid forecaster, since it peeks at the quarter being predicted, but a useful upper bound — improves on the best lagged model by only 2.2% in mean absolute error, far short of the pre-registered 20% threshold, so H2 is not supported. A state-block bootstrap of that gap puts its 95% confidence interval at [−4.6%, +8.5%], which includes zero, so even the same-quarter "oracle" stressors do not reliably beat the best lagged model. At this resolution the surveyed signal is neither leading nor dependably coincident.

### 4.4 Corroboration

The conclusion holds up under the secondary analyses (Figure 3). In an expanding-window rolling-origin evaluation over test years 2020 through 2025, the loss-only model stays below one in every year, while adding stressors helps in some years and hurts in others with no consistent pattern. State by state, the loss-only model beats the seasonal-naive baseline in 62% of states and the loss-plus-stressors model in 58%, but adding the stressors improves on loss-only in only 53% of states, which is indistinguishable from a coin flip. Removing the COVID-affected quarters of 2020 and 2021 from training barely moves the headline (0.934 to 0.916), so the result is not an artifact of the pandemic-era disruption.

## 5. Power and limitations

A null result is only as strong as the test's ability to detect a real effect, so we state that ability directly. Under the pre-registered primary state-block bootstrap, the minimum effect this design could reliably detect is about 0.07–0.09 MASE (2.8 standard errors; 0.089 for the headline integer-target gradient-boosted model). The secondary period-block bootstrap gives roughly 0.06, and the penalized-linear contrast, which is less variable, about 0.02. We headline the larger, primary figure so as not to understate the underpowering: with on the order of ten effective temporal units, and with state coverage in the test window thinning from 45 states in 2023Q1–2024Q2 to 21 in 2024Q3–2025Q2 as NASS reduced its sample, genuinely small leading effects would be invisible to us. The claim is therefore the absence of a usable leading signal at state-by-quarter resolution, not evidence that no relationship exists.

Two further caveats bound the interpretation. First, even the best learner's advantage over the trivial recalibrated-naive baseline is only about 0.025 MASE, below the period-block detection threshold; strictly, the best lagged model is indistinguishable from a recalibrated baseline, which reinforces rather than competes with the null. Second, the result is about the marginal out-of-sample forecasting value of a coarse, noisy survey measure, and says nothing about whether Varroa, pesticides, or disease *cause* colony loss. Lagged loss itself partly encodes past stressor exposure, so a forecaster that ignores the stressor columns is not ignoring stressor biology. The remaining limitations are familiar ones: the published target is integer-rounded, though we replicate on a continuous reconstruction; the resolution is coarse; the historic 2024–25 loss event sits only partly inside a short test window; and the weather and land-cover covariates of H3 are deferred for want of a public release that overlaps the test years.

## 6. Related work

The closest explanatory work is Insolia et al. (2022, *Scientific Reports*), which fits in-sample associations on this exact NASS panel augmented with weather and land cover; we take the same data and ask the forecasting question their design was not built to answer, and find that the associations do not transfer out of sample. On the predictive side, Calovi et al. (2021) and Gray, Goslee, Kammerer & Grozinger (2024, *Journal of Insect Science*) fit random forests but use k-fold cross-validation and explicitly decline the temporal validation we run. The same group has also deployed BeeWinterWise, a real-time overwintering-survival predictor within the Beescape platform and the nearest existing operational forecaster in this space; it is weather-driven, operates at the apiary level, and predicts a binary survive-or-die outcome, and to our knowledge it too has not been evaluated under a temporal train-past/test-future split, so it does not pre-empt the state-level pre-registered floor we establish. A separate strand forecasts colony loss from in-hive sensor streams (for example a temporal convolutional network published in *Sensors* in 2021), but that operates on individual instrumented hives and addresses a different problem. The conceptual frame for all of this is the distinction between explanatory and predictive modeling set out by Shmueli (2010).

## 7. Conclusion

At state-by-quarter resolution, the stressor prevalences that the field treats as drivers of colony loss do not provide a usable leading signal for forecasting it. The modest predictability that exists is recalibration of a seasonal baseline rather than information about stressors or even about recent loss. A strong in-sample fit did not survive a pre-registered out-of-sample test. We do not read this as a verdict against the biology, which our design cannot speak to, but as a measurement result about what this particular survey can and cannot forecast at this resolution. We release the panel and the benchmark so that the floor can be raised deliberately, with finer spatial or temporal resolution, sensor data, or covariates, and tested against a fixed, pre-registered baseline rather than an in-sample fit.

## Reproducibility and data availability

The repository contains the code, the locked pre-registration with its deviations log, every result table, and the released panel. After `pip install -r requirements.txt`, the command sequence `python -m src.fetch_nass --confirm-prereg-locked`, `python -m src.build_panel`, `python -m src.analyze`, and `python -m src.robustness` reproduces every number in this paper, and `python -m src.figures` regenerates the figures; the analysis also runs offline against the committed panel without an API key. The derived panel is released under CC0, the underlying NASS data being in the public domain.
